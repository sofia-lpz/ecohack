About Fertex

Fertex provides calculations of N₂O emissions for fertilizers. Nitrous oxide is a gas that has a global warming potential of 298, which means that it is 298 times more effective at trapping heat in the atmosphere compared to carbon dioxide (CO₂) over a 100-year period. This high global warming potential makes N₂O a significant contributor to climate change and a significant component in some fertilizers.

For the calculations on the weight and percentage of nitrogen in the fertilizer, Fertex uses the following equation:

CO2 equialent Emissions = Amount of Fertilizer x Nitrogen Content Percentage per kg of Fertilizer x Emission Factor (EF1) x Global Warming Potential of N2O



Where the Emission Factor is the one provided by the IPCC in its 2019 Refinement to the 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories (Volume 4, Chapter 11, Table 11.1). This is a Tier 1 Emission Factor “for N additions from mineral fertilizers, organic amendments, and crop residues, and N mineralized from mineral soil as a result of loss of soil carbon [kg N₂O–N (kg N)⁻¹]” or, in other words, the coefficient that represents the amount of nitrous oxide emissions per unit of nitrogen added to the soil.

Emission factors are coefficients that represent the quantity of a pollutant released into the atmosphere per unit of the activity.

For the historical data, Fertex considers category 3.D.1 as proposed by the IPCC: Direct N₂O emissions from managed soils.
